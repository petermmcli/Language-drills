Grammar

1. 进去 / 进来 — V + 去/来 Directional Complement

Attaching 去 or 来 to a directional verb specifies whether motion moves away from or toward the speaker. Structure: Verb + 去 (away) / Verb + 来 (toward). In the perfective, 了 follows the whole complement (我进去了, not *我进了去).

我进去了。🔊
Wǒ jìnqù le.
I went in (away from you).
请进来！🔊
Qǐng jìnlái!
Please come in (toward me)!
别进去！🔊
Bié jìnqù!
Don't go in!

2. Adjective + 了 — Change of State

When 了 follows an adjective (not a verb), it signals a new state — the subject has just become (more) [adjective]. Think of it as "now X" or "got X-er." This is the sentence-final change-of-state 了, distinct from the perfective 了 after verbs. Structure: Subject + Adjective + 了.

他渴了。🔊
Tā kě le.
He's gotten thirsty.
西瓜甜了。🔊
Xīguā tián le.
The watermelon has turned sweet.
我累了，不跑步了。🔊
Wǒ lèi le, bù pǎobù le.
I'm tired now — I'm not running anymore.

3. A + 和 + B + 一起 — "A and B Together"

Expresses A and B doing something together. Key point: 一起 (yìqǐ, "together") comes before the verb in Chinese, not after. Structure: A + 和 + B + 一起 + Verb. Add 吧 to soften into a proposal.

我和你一起去吧。🔊
Wǒ hé nǐ yìqǐ qù ba.
Let's go together.
我和爷爷一起吃饭。🔊
Wǒ hé yéye yìqǐ chīfàn.
I'll eat with grandpa.
他们和老板一起工作。🔊
Tāmen hé lǎobǎn yìqǐ gōngzuò.
They work together with the boss.

4. 帮 + Person + Verb — "Help Someone Do X"

帮 (bāng) takes the person as its direct object — no preposition needed. The action verb follows immediately after the person, meaning the helper performs the action on their behalf. Add 一下 to soften into a polite offer. Structure: Subject + 帮 + Person + Verb (+ Object).

我帮你找一下。🔊
Wǒ bāng nǐ zhǎo yíxià.
Let me help you look for it.
你帮我买一瓶水吧。🔊
Nǐ bāng wǒ mǎi yì píng shuǐ ba.
Please buy a bottle of water for me.
妈妈帮我做作业。🔊
Māma bāng wǒ zuò zuòyè.
Mom helps me with my homework.

5. 找 + Person + Amount — Giving Back Change

In commercial contexts, 找 (zhǎo) means "to give back as change" — completely different from its other meaning "to look for." The recipient is the direct object; the amount follows with 块 (colloquial) or 元 (formal). Structure: 找 + Person + Number + 块/元.

找您25块。🔊
Zhǎo nín èrshíwǔ kuài.
Here's 25 kuai change.
我找你5块。🔊
Wǒ zhǎo nǐ wǔ kuài.
I'll give you back 5 kuai.
一共十块，找您两块。🔊
Yígòng shí kuài, zhǎo nín liǎng kuài.
Ten kuai total; here's 2 kuai change.

6. 一点儿 vs 有点儿 — "A Bit" (Comparative vs Complaint)

Two ways to say "a bit" with opposite placements and different nuances. 有点儿 + Adjective = "kind of / a bit [adj]" — usually carries a negative or unwanted nuance. Adjective + 一点儿 = "a little [adj]-er" — neutral comparative. Quick test: if you can say "kind of," use 有点儿; if you can say "a bit more," use 一点儿.

西瓜有点儿贵。🔊
Xīguā yǒu diǎnr guì.
The watermelon is kind of expensive.
我要一个大一点儿的。🔊
Wǒ yào yí ge dà yìdiǎnr de.
I want a bigger one (a bit bigger).
这个有点儿甜，那个甜一点儿。🔊
Zhèi ge yǒu diǎnr tián, nèi ge tián yìdiǎnr.
This one's a bit too sweet; that one's a bit sweeter.

7. 所有的 + Noun — "All the [Noun]"

Emphasizes the entirety of a group — "all OF the X." More emphatic than 都 alone. Typically reinforced by 都 in the predicate, stressing that no member is excluded. Structure: 所有的 + Noun + 都 + Predicate.

所有的苹果都很甜。🔊
Suǒyǒude píngguǒ dōu hěn tián.
All the apples are sweet.
我吃了所有的水果。🔊
Wǒ chī le suǒyǒude shuǐguǒ.
I ate all the fruit.
所有的店员都很热情。🔊
Suǒyǒude diànyuán dōu hěn rèqíng.
All the shop assistants are very friendly.

8. Subject (Plural) + 都 — "All / Every"

都 (dōu) goes after the subject and before the verb or adjective, indicating that the predicate applies to every member of the subject. Required whenever a plural subject means "all" or "every." Unlike English ("they all like"), Chinese attaches "all" to the verb side via 都. Structure: Subject (plural) + 都 + Verb/Adjective.

他们都喜欢吃水果。🔊
Tāmen dōu xǐhuan chī shuǐguǒ.
They all like eating fruit.
苹果和西瓜都很甜。🔊
Píngguǒ hé xīguā dōu hěn tián.
Apples and watermelon are both sweet.
这些便利店都很方便。🔊
Zhèi xiē biànlìdiàn dōu hěn fāngbiàn.
All these convenience stores are very convenient.
